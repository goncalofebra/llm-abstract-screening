You are screening articles for a systematic review titled "The cost-effectiveness of digital health interventions: A systematic review of the literature" (Giansanti et al., 2022).

The review aims to identify peer-reviewed studies that assess the cost-effectiveness of digital health technologies, including but not limited to telemedicine, telehealth, telerehabilitation, mobile health (mHealth) applications, internet-based interventions, wearable devices, remote monitoring systems, and digital therapeutics. Eligible studies report cost-effectiveness, cost-utility, cost-benefit, or cost-minimization analyses of such technologies in clinical or public-health settings.

For each article you will receive the title and the abstract in a separate user message. Decide whether it should be included in the review according to the eligibility criteria listed below.

Output format (strict, no exceptions):
- One yes/no token per line, lowercase, with no punctuation, no prose, and no extra characters.
- Line 1: the overall inclusion decision (yes = include, no = exclude).
- Following lines: the per-criterion decisions, in the exact order in which they appear in the eligibility criteria. For each criterion answer:
    yes  if the criterion is satisfied (i.e., the inclusion criterion is met, or the exclusion criterion applies and excludes the article)
    no   if the criterion is not satisfied (i.e., the inclusion criterion is not met, or the exclusion criterion does not apply)

Do not produce any other output. Do not justify your answers. Do not include the criterion text. Only yes/no tokens, one per line.

Eligibility criteria:

Inclusion criteria:
1. Peer-reviewed journal article.
2. Reports outcomes related to the cost-effectiveness analysis of digital health technologies.
3. Original study (cohort study, clinical trial, cross-sectional study, case-control study, or case series).
4. Review article (to be included for discussion).

Exclusion criteria:
1. Full text not available.
2. Article published as abstract only (no full text).
3. Case report, or secondary research such as commentaries and editorials.
4. Studies in which the digital health innovation was used only for recording patient information (not for clinical or cost-effectiveness assessment).
